(1) Introduction

Problem Statement
Kuwait University students regularly require guidance on academic procedures, administrative processes, and campus-related services throughout their student life. Although this information is available through official platforms and university offices, it is often dispersed across multiple sources, making it difficult for students to quickly locate accurate and relevant answers. As a result, many students repeatedly seek assistance from academic and administrative offices across the university for common inquiries, increasing operational pressure on staff and limiting the efficiency of existing support mechanisms.
This project proposes the development of an AI-enabled chatbot designed to enhance access to university information from multiple credible sources. The chatbot will provide general guidance applicable to all Kuwait University students, while offering more detailed and structured academic information for the College of Life Sciences (CLS) students, in alignment with the project scope and available domain knowledge. By centralizing essential university-wide and CLS-specific information within a single intelligent platform, the chatbot improves accessibility, reduces repetitive inquiries, and represents a practical and sustainable application of artificial intelligence aimed at improving efficiency and student experience within Kuwait University.
Project Objectives
The primary objective of this project is to develop Daleel KU, an AI-enabled bilingual chatbot that provides Kuwait University students with accurate, centralized access to academic and administrative information, reducing reliance on informal channels and repetitive inquiries to staff.
The specific objectives are as follows:
1. Develop a RAG-based system that grounds all responses in verified official KU sources with source transparency.
2. Support bilingual interaction in Arabic and English. 3. Provide university-wide information support covering admission, academic procedures,
university policies, and campus services for specific colleges. 4. Deliver enhanced academic guidance for College of Life Sciences students, including
course prerequisites, sequencing, and GPA estimation.
pg. 6

5. Build an automated content ingestion pipeline that processes KU websites, PDFs, and social media into a searchable knowledge base.
6. Provide an administrative control panel for knowledge base management, query log monitoring, and system statistics.
7. Ensure accessibility through high contrast mode, screen reader support, voice-to-text input, and keyboard navigation.
System Scope and Justification
System Scope
Daleel KU serves four user groups: prospective students, enrolled KU students, CLS students, and authorized administrative staff. All users receive general university-wide support including guidance on academic procedures, admission requirements, and university policies. CLS students additionally receive course-specific guidance, prerequisite information, course sequencing, and GPA estimation.
The following are explicitly out of scope: private student academic records, official academic advising, and detailed department-specific guidance for colleges other than CLS. The system does not replace human advisors for complex cases and makes no official academic commitments on behalf of Kuwait University. The architecture is designed to support expansion to additional colleges in future enhancements.
Justification
University information is currently fragmented across multiple platforms, forcing students to rely on informal channels and placing repetitive load on administrative staff. A RAG approach was chosen over purely generative AI to ground responses in verified official KU content, ensuring accuracy and traceability to official university sources. Bilingual support in Arabic and English is justified by the presence of non-Arabic-speaking students within KU's student population, ensuring equitable access for all users. The CLS scope reflects the availability of verified academic data within the project timeline. The decision to develop Daleel KU was further validated through a student survey conducted across multiple Kuwait University colleges prior to development. The results confirmed that the challenges this system aims to address are widely experienced, and that

students expressed genuine interest in an AI-based solution, with a usage likelihood rating of 4.35 out of 5.
Global and Local Effects of the System
The deployment of Daleel KU produces measurable effects at both the local institutional level and the broader regional and global level. These effects span academic, administrative, technological, and social dimensions.
Benefits for Students
· Providing a single centralized platform that combines academic and administrative information related to Kuwait University.
· Enabling continuous access to information at any time, without dependence on office hours or staff availability.
· Supporting bilingual interaction in Arabic and English, allowing students to receive information in their preferred language.
· Assisting students in understanding academic procedures such as admission requirements, course registration processes and other administrative procedures.
· Reducing information-seeking effort and confusion caused by having multiple websites, social media accounts, and informal communication channels.
Benefits for Kuwait University Staff
· Reducing the load of repetitive routine inquiries directed to administrative and academic staff.
· Ensuring consistency and alignment of responses with official university policies and approved sources.
· Minimizing the spread of misinformation and contradictory guidance across departments. · Allowing staff members to focus on complex cases that require human judgment and
individualized support.

Regional and Global Impact
At the regional level, Daleel KU represents one of the first implemented bilingual AI academic RAG-based chatbots designed specifically for a Kuwaiti university. The system shows that academic AI tools can be built reliably using modern retrieval-augmented generation technology including bilingual support for Arabic and English, providing a replicable model for other universities across Kuwait and the broader Gulf Cooperation Council region.
At the global level, the project contributes to the growing body of work on domain-specific conversational AI systems in higher education. The system's design addresses challenges that are relevant beyond Kuwait, including cross-lingual semantic retrieval between Arabic and English, transparent uncertainty handling through source citation, and human-in-the-loop governance through an administrative control panel. These design decisions reflect internationally recognized best practices in responsible AI deployment and could inform similar implementations at universities in other Arabic-speaking countries or multilingual institutional environments worldwide.
From a social impact perspective, Daleel KU promotes equity in information access within Kuwait University by ensuring that all students, regardless of their language preference, social network, or familiarity with university systems, have equal access to accurate and timely academic information.
Related Work and Evidence Studies
The use of AI-based chatbots in higher education has grown significantly in recent years, with universities worldwide deploying conversational systems to assist students with academic advising, registration, campus services, and technical support. This trend reflects a broader recognition that centralized, intelligent information systems can reduce administrative burden and improve student experience on a scale. Within Kuwait, several university chatbot implementations have emerged that are directly relevant to this project:

Overview of Existing Solutions
AUK Admission Chatbot The American University of Kuwait developed a chatbot to support prospective students by
providing information about programs, admission requirements, application procedures, eligibility checks, frequently asked questions, and personalized program recommendations [1].
FalconAI: Custom University Chatbot (ChatGPT API) FalconAI is a customizable AI chatbot developed for Gulf University of Science and
Technology (GUST) in Kuwait, designed to provide quick reference and support services to students and faculty at any time. The system is built using Python and the ChatGPT API [2].
Kuwait University Research Sector Projects Unit (RSPU) Chatbot Kuwait University has a deployed AI assistant on the Research Sector Projects Unit portal.
The assistant provides information about RSPU's scientific research facilities, available analytical instruments, ongoing research projects, and sample analysis request procedures. It also explains details about scientific devices such as spectrometers, chromatographs, and elemental analyzers [3].
AUKbot: IT Support AI Virtual Assistant AUKbot is an AI-enabled chatbot developed for technical support services at the American
University of Kuwait. The system addresses technical queries from students, staff, and faculty, reducing the workload on the existing IT helpdesk [4].
Identified Gaps and Motivation
The review of existing systems highlights that while AI-based chatbots have been successfully deployed in various university contexts in Kuwait, each of the reviewed systems was developed to serve a specific and narrow institutional function, whether admissions processing, IT technical support or research facility inquiries. None of the reviewed systems were designed to serve the broad range of academic and administrative needs of Kuwait University students in a unified platform.
This gap motivated the development of Daleel KU. Kuwait University students require a single system that can address general university information, respond in the student's preferred
pg. 10

language, and ground every response in official university sources. CLS students additionally benefit from course prerequisites, GPA estimation, and campus services specific to their college. No existing local system addresses this combination of needs. Daleel KU was designed specifically to fill this gap, offering a unified, bilingual, source-transparent, and institutionally governed academic assistant for Kuwait University students, with the architecture designed to expand to additional colleges in future enhancements.

Dependencies and Constraints
Dependencies a. Official University Data Sources: The chatbot depends on access to accurate and up-todate information from official Kuwait University sources, including the KU website, student handbooks, course catalogs, and College of Life Sciences resources. Content is pre-ingested and stored in the system's own knowledge base rather than queried live, meaning the chatbot remains functional even if official websites experience downtime or structural changes. Authorized administrators are responsible for periodically refreshing the knowledge base through the administrative panel to ensure content remains current. b. Digital Infrastructure and technical tools: The development relies on AI frameworks, natural language processing libraries, and web development technologies compatible with both desktop and mobile environments. In addition, a stable internet connection is required to use the system. c. Administrative Oversight: The system depends on authorized KU staff to oversee content, manage data ingestion, and validate updates as part of the human-in-the-loop governance process. d. Student and User Engagement: Successful use of the chatbot depends on students and prospective users actively engaging with the system and providing feedback to improve functionality.
Constraints a. Scope Limitation: The system provides university-wide information to high school students seeking admission and to students enrolled in other colleges at KU. Detailed academic guidance and planning support are offered only to College of Life Sciences students. b. Data Availability: The chatbot can only access information that is publicly available or provided through authorized channels, it cannot access private academic records or restricted administrative data.

pg. 14

c. Data Sources Reliability: The chatbot's responses rely on the accuracy and consistency of the information from official university sources. In cases of missing data, the system transparently communicates the uncertainty to the user and tells them to refer to official sources.
d. User Input Quality: The chatbot's understanding depends on users entering clear, grammatically reasonable queries. Ambiguous or highly complex questions may reduce accuracy. A character limit of 1,000 characters per query is enforced to maintain response quality and prevent prompt injection attempts.
e. Legal and Ethical Considerations: The chatbot protects user privacy by ensuring that any personal information, such as grades or other sensitive data provided by one user, is not shared with other users. The system must also avoid providing misleading information or making admission guarantees. No personally identifiable information is collected or stored. Query logs record only the submitted query text, the generated response, and a timestamp, with no link to any individual user identity.

Data Collection Relevant information was gathered from official Kuwait University sources including the KU
website, student handbooks, and CLS-specific materials. All collected content is chunked, embedded, and stored in the Supabase pgvector knowledge base. System Design and Architecture
The overall system architecture was designed, including the chatbot core logic, retrieval mechanism, backend structure, integration workflow, and administrative control considerations, these will be discussed in detail in Section 4 of the report.
Frontend UI Development The web-based user interface was developed using React, TypeScript, Tailwind CSS, and
Vite. The student-facing interface includes an animated landing page, a bilingual chat interface with voice-to-text input, source citations attached to responses, typing indicators, high contrast mode, and keyboard navigation. The administrative panel includes a login page, dashboard with real-time statistics, content management page, and query logs page with session grouping and CSV export.
Chatbot Core Logic and Retrieval System The chatbot's natural language processing capabilities and information retrieval mechanism
were implemented. This includes query classification, Arabic text normalization, semantic vector search, context assembly, response generation with language mirroring, source citation, and fallback handling for unanswered queries. Data ingestion pipeline was implemented to support three source types: web pages, PDF documents, and social media content. All ingested content is chunked and stored persistently in the database. Backend and Full Integration
The backend was developed to handle all student and administrative API requests. It manages session creation, query routing, RAG pipeline invocation, GPA estimation, admin authentication, knowledge base management, and anonymous query logging.

Application of Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC)

This project followed an Iterative Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) model, which allows progressive refinement through repeated cycles of planning, development, and evaluation. The iterative approach was chosen because it enables building a functional prototype early, gather feedback, and make improvements in subsequent iterations. This was particularly suitable for this project, as the chatbot system required continuous refinement of the knowledge base, response accuracy, and user interface based on testing and evaluation outcomes.

Requirement Elicitation Techniques and Strategies
Document Analysis Document analysis served as a foundational requirement elicitation technique that
established the baseline for this project. This method was chosen because official university resources provide accurate, verified information and reflect how students currently access services. The following resources were analyzed:
1. The official Kuwait University website and academic portal 2. Official Kuwait University social media channels 3. Existing chatbot systems at other universities (see Related Work Section) Through conducting document analysis, several insights were identified. Information is scattered across multiple platforms, making it difficult for students to find what they need. Critical updates such as schedule changes are only announced through social media accounts rather than official portals. Additionally, the student handbook, a primary resource for students, is only available in Arabic, creating a gap for non-Arabic speaking students. These findings justified the need for a centralized, bilingual, conversational system.
Supervisor Consultation Our consultations with the project supervisor, Dr. Bader Alkhazi, were treated as a formal
elicitation technique during the beginning of planning and requirements gathering phases. These sessions were structured around scope clarification and directional feedback on system boundaries. This ensured that the requirements being gathered remain academically realistic within the capstone timeline, and responsive to the standards expected of a formal system specification.
Interview A structured interview was held, this technique was chosen because, unlike surveys,
interviews allow for open-ended exploration, follow-up questioning, and the possible discovery of implicit requirements.
pg. 20

Vice Dean of Students' Affairs The Vice-Dean of Students' Affairs, Dr. Sharifa Alkandari, was identified as the most suitable
consultation given her office's oversight of student-facing administrative services at the institutional level. While students are often directed to Dr. Sharifa's office for matters that require higher-level authorization or administrative escalation in College of Life Sciences, many students also approach her office directly for broader administrative concerns, making her office a comprehensive source of insight into the full range of student administrative needs. The interview with Dr. Sharifa provided us with insights:
· Most common student inquiries: Transfer rules, course information, GPA-related questions, academic warnings, graduation requirements.
· On digital channels: Dr. Sharifa confirmed that any question involving a written university rule can be answered digitally. However, students tend to rely on unofficial WhatsApp groups which frequently contain inaccurate content, directly validating the core motivation behind Daleel KU.
· Recommended sources: College and department official websites, the Vice Dean's and Dean of Admission and Registration's official social media accounts, and department chairs for course-specific information.
· Chatbot boundaries: Questions about case-specific academic records, insufficient information should always be redirected to the appropriate office and the GPA estimation should only be advisory.
· Expectations: The chatbot's information must be verified and reliable so students naturally shift away from informal channels.
Survey-Based Validation A student survey was conducted to gather broad quantitative input directly from the student
population (see Appendix A for survey questions and survey results). The survey was distributed early to capture student perspectives on information-seeking behavior and chatbot feature preferences across Kuwait University's colleges. Approximately, 50 responses were collected from students across multiple colleges, with the majority of respondents from the College of Life Sciences, followed by Engineering and Petroleum, Medicine, Business Administration, and Allied Health Sciences.
pg. 21

The survey results strongly validated the core problem statement of this project. When asked about the challenges they face when searching for university information, the majority of respondents identified information being scattered across multiple platforms as their primary challenge, followed by long processes to find answers, outdated information, and having to contact multiple departments. Furthermore, when asked where they first look for academic or administrative information, students ranked friends and classmates as their most used source, followed by official social media accounts and WhatsApp groups, with the official KU website ranking only fourth. The overall results confirm that students face difficulty finding information and rely more on informal channels for information.
Regarding the proposed chatbot, students expressed strong interest with an average usage likelihood rating of 4.35 out of 5. The most requested topics were registration, campus services, GPA calculation, prerequisite courses, university policies, and equivalent courses, directly validating the core use cases defined in this report. Additionally, students rated source transparency as important with an average rating of 4.52 out of 5, confirming the decision to include source references alongside chatbot responses as a key feature.

Design Modeling
Design Strategies
Designing a system that is easy to build, maintain, and scale requires deliberate and thoughtful decisions from the start. The following strategies outline the approach taken to ensure Daleel KU remains structured, manageable, and adaptable throughout its development lifecycle.
Design Approach The system follows a top-down design approach, starting with a broad view of what the system
needs to do and working down into the details. Rather than jumping straight into individual components, the chatbot was first defined as a complete system, one that answers student queries, and then broken down into subsystems: data collection, processing, storage, backend logic, and frontend interface.
Design Paradigm A modular design paradigm was chosen to keep things focused and straightforward. Each part
of the system is responsible for one specific task, whether it is scraping websites or social media content, extracting content from PDFs, managing document chunking and retrieval, or handling API requests.
Design Benefits Modularity: meaning that the system is divided into self-contained components, each with a clear responsibility. They communicate through well-defined interfaces, so one component can be updated, debugged, or replaced without touching the others. This makes troubleshooting simpler and leaves room for future additions, like new data sources or a different language model. Reusability: which cuts down on duplicated effort and keeps behavior consistent across the system. The document processing pipeline works the same way regardless of where the content comes from, and the same logic handles embedding generation and search whether the source is a website, a PDF, or a social media post. This consistency reduces redundancy and makes the system easier to maintain over time.
pg. 48

Security Strategies
The Academic Chatbot system is designed as an open-access platform, meaning no authentication is required from general student users. However, since the system handles academic information, processes user queries, and includes a privileged administrative control panel, a layered security approach has been adopted across all components of the system. The following security measures define how the system protects data, controls access, and maintains integrity throughout its operation:
Authentication and Authorization Access to the administrative control panel is restricted to authorized Kuwait University staff
through Supabase Auth. Upon successful login, Supabase issues a JSON Web Token (JWT) that is stored client-side and included in subsequent requests to the Flask backend. The backend verifies the token on each request by calling Supabase's get_user API, ensuring that only authenticated administrators can access functions such as knowledge base management and query log review. Unauthorized users attempting to access the admin panel are denied access and presented with an authentication error.
Session Management Administrative sessions are governed by JWT expiration configured in Supabase Auth.
After the token expires, the admin must re-authenticate to regain access.
Transport Layer Security When the system is deployed to a production environment, all communication between the
client and the server will be conducted over HTTPS, ensuring that data transmitted between the user's device and the system is encrypted in transit.
Rate Limiting To protect the system from excessive or abusive usage, rate limiting is applied to all public
query endpoints using Flask-Limiter, restricting each client to 30 requests per minute per IP address.
pg. 49

Input Validation and Injection Prevention All user inputs submitted through the chat interface are validated and sanitized on the
server side before processing. A 1,000-character limit is enforced on all queries. Inputs are sanitized by removing hidden characters that could interfere with backend processing.
· SQL injection is mitigated across all database operations through Supabase's client library, which uses parameterized queries by default, ensuring that user input is never directly embedded into database queries.
· Prompt injection is mitigated by maintaining a strict separation between the system prompt and user input within all LLM API calls, each is passed as a distinct message role, preventing user text from overriding system instructions.
Data Privacy and Anonymized Logging Since the user-facing interface operates without user authentication, no personally identifiable
information is collected, stored, or linked to any interaction. Query logs maintained by the system record the submitted query text, the generated response, whether the query was successfully answered, and a timestamp. No information that identifies the individual user is stored.
Integrity
Integrity ensures that the system's software and data remain accurate, consistent, and trustworthy throughout operation. The following subsections describe how integrity is maintained at each level.
Security-Based Integrity The security measures described in the previous section play a direct role in maintaining
system integrity. Authentication and authorization ensure that only authorized administrators can modify the knowledge base, preventing unauthorized changes to system content. Transport layer security ensures that data transmitted between the user interface and backend services cannot be altered during transit when deployed to a production environment. Input validation and sanitization prevent malicious inputs from corrupting stored data.
pg. 50

Software Integrity Software integrity focuses on ensuring the system's codebase remains reliable, secure, and
free from unauthorized modifications. GitHub is used as the version control system, which allows all code changes to be tracked, documented, and reviewed before being merged into the main codebase. This approach provides a complete history of modifications and enables reverting to previous versions if issues arise. The system follows a change management process where proposed modifications are reviewed and approved before being merged, preventing unplanned changes from affecting system stability.
Data Integrity Data integrity ensures that information stored and processed by the system remains accurate,
consistent, and trustworthy. When users submit queries, the system validates the input before processing to ensure that empty, excessively long, or malformed queries are rejected. The chatbot knowledge base is sourced exclusively from official Kuwait University channels, including the KU website, KU portal, and verified social media accounts, ensuring that all information provided to students is accurate and institutionally approved. Each document added to the knowledge base is timestamped to track when it was uploaded or last updated, allowing content to remain current and traceable to its origin. Only authenticated administrators can modify the knowledge base, while students interact with the system through a read-only interface. All ingested content whether from web pages, PDF documents, or social media posts goes through a structured ingestion pipeline that chunks, normalizes, and embeds the content before storing it in the Supabase pgvector knowledge base, ensuring consistency across all knowledge base entries.

Configuration and Supabase Client Design All core settings and shared components including the OpenAI language model, the
embedding model, and the database clients are initialized once in config.py and shared across the entire backend. This ensures consistency and avoids redundant initialization across files.

Two separate Supabase clients are used. The first, supabase, uses the public anon key and is used only for verifying admin login tokens. The second, supabase_admin, uses the service role key and is used for all database operations such as reading and writing documents and query logs. This separation is necessary because the service role key bypasses Supabase's Row Level Security (RLS), which allows the backend to read and write to the database freely on the server side, while the anon key remains restricted as intended for client-facing authentication.

RAG Pipeline
The Daleel KU chatbot uses a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipeline to answer student questions. Before the pipeline is invoked, each query is classified as either a general academic question or a GPA estimation request and routed accordingly.
Pipeline Flow A query passes through the following stages in sequence:
1. Input Validation: Empty strings and queries exceeding 1,000 characters are rejected with a 400 error.
2. Query Classification: The query is submitted to GPT-4o which returns either "gpa" or "general".
3. Routing: GPA queries skip to Step 6; General queries continue to Step 4.
4. Embedding and Retrieval: The student's question undergoes Arabic text normalization, which removes kashida (), a decorative elongation character, and diacritics (), short vowel markers, since their presence or absence in different texts causes the same word to produce inconsistent embeddings. Subscript digits are also normalized to standard digits. The normalized question is then embedded using text-embedding-3-small and compared against the pgvector knowledge base via similarity search. The top five most relevant chunks are retrieved, and duplicate chunks are removed to ensure each piece of content appears only once in the context. Optional metadata filters by college_id and topic_id can be applied to scope retrieval to a specific college or topic, allowing more targeted results when the query context warrants it.
5. Context Assembly: Retrieved chunks are assembled into a context string labeled with source page numbers. Empty context triggers the fallback path, returning a bilingual fallback message.
6. Response Generation: GPA queries are handled directly using the KU grade scale without retrieval. LaTeX notation present in the generated GPA response is stripped before the answer is returned to the client, ensuring the student receives clean readable text. General queries are passed to GPT-4o alongside the retrieved context and a grounding prompt that
pg. 99

enforces language mirroring, ensuring the response is generated in the same language as the student's question.

7. JSON Parsing and Flag Assignment: GPT-4o returns {"was_answered": bool, "answer": str}. If parsing fails, the raw text is surfaced and was_answered defaults to false.

8. Logging: The query, response, and was_answered flag are logged anonymously to Supabase. The session_id is included only if provided by the client. Logging failures do not block the response.

9. Response Delivery: The final JSON payload containing response, was_answered, source_url, and source_name is returned with HTTP 200. Source metadata is included when context was successfully retrieved; the frontend determines whether to display it based on the was_answered flag.

RAG Components The pipeline is implemented across five modules, with the IngestionPipeline, QueryEngine,
and ResponseGenerator classes in the class diagram representing the three core logical components of the pipeline. The routes/chat.py implements the QueryProcessor logic that classifies and routes each query before the pipeline is invoked.

Knowledge Base

Data Sources The Daleel KU knowledge base is designed to ingest content from three different source types, as

specified in the system design data ingestion architecture:

1. Web Pages Content from official Kuwait University websites is crawled and extracted using Firecrawl. The number of pages crawled per source is controlled by the crawl_depth setting, which determines whether to crawl a single page, half of all discoverable URLs, or the full domain. If college_id is not pre-set on the source row, the first crawled page is sent to GPT-4o to auto-classify the college. PDF links discovered during the crawl are collected and processed through the same ingestion pipeline used for admin-uploaded PDFs. Staging mirror domains are filtered out before ingestion, and previously ingested pages are skipped to prevent duplicates.

2. PDF Documents Official Kuwait University publications are extracted using LlamaParse, which was selected after PyMuPDF was found to corrupt Arabic decimal numbers in RTL PDFs. LlamaParse is called with Arabic language support enabled and parsed content is cached locally so unchanged PDFs are never re-parsed. Admin-uploaded PDFs are stored in Supabase Storage before ingestion, while

pg. 101

PDFs discovered during web crawls are downloaded, uploaded to storage, and cleaned up after processing.

3. Social Media: Announcements and updates from official KU Instagram accounts are scraped using Apify. Up to 20 recent posts per account are collected. Each post image is sent to GPT-4o Vision for OCRbased text extraction, which is then merged with the post caption into a single content string before ingestion. No images are stored in the system.

Vector Storage Embeddings are stored permanently in the Supabase chunk table using pgvector, allowing the
knowledge base to grow incrementally as administrators add new documents without requiring a full rebuild on every restart. To reduce LlamaParse API calls, parsed document content is cached locally so LlamaParse is only called when new documents are added.

These interfaces have been implemented, and the application runs in the browser communicating with the Flask backend through API calls.

Database Implementation
Supabase has been implemented as the system's database and authentication platform, providing a hosted PostgreSQL database, a built-in authentication service, and a file storage bucket. The database schema consists of nine tables covering three main areas: logging anonymous student interactions, managing admin content and oversight, and storing the RAG knowledge base. The storage bucket is used exclusively for PDF files. For admin-uploaded PDFs, the bucket serves as the primary storage and its public URL is used as the document source reference. For scraperingested PDFs, the bucket stores a backup copy while the original KU URL is retained as the source reference shown to students.

Code Correctness, Robustness, and Security
Input Validation
All student queries pass through a validation function before any processing takes place. This
ensures that invalid inputs are rejected immediately, before they reach the AI pipeline or consume
any API credits as OpenAI charges based on the number of words and characters sent in each
request. Without a limit, a single unusually long query could consume a disproportionate amount
of API credits. Therefore, two checks are applied:
Check 1 Empty Input: Queries that are empty or contain only whitespace are rejected. This
prevents meaningless requests from reaching the query classification step and the OpenAI API.
Check 2 Length Limit: Queries exceeding 1,000 characters are rejected.
The validation logic is implemented in validate_query() in routes/chat.py:
MAX_QUERY_LENGTH = 1000 # characters
# Validates the student query before processing def validate_query(text):
# Reject empty or whitespace-only input if not text or not text.strip():
pg. 107

return False, "Query can't be empty."
# Reject queries exceeding the character limit to prevent prompt injection if len(text) > MAX_QUERY_LENGTH:
return False, f"Query is too long. Maximum allowed length is {MAX_QUERY_LENGTH} characters."
return True, None
If either check fails, the route immediately returns HTTP 400 with a descriptive error message and no further processing occurs:
is_valid, error_message = validate_query(query_text) if not is_valid:
return jsonify({"error": error_message}), 400
Error Handling
The system is designed to handle failures such that when something goes wrong internally, the student still receives an appropriate response and is never shown a raw technical error.
Classification Failure Fallback When classifying a query as GPA-related or general, the system calls GPT-4o with a short
classification prompt. If this call fails for any reason such as a network timeout or API error, the system does not raise an exception. Instead, it silently defaults to the general query type and continues processing normally. This ensures that a classification failure never blocks the student from receiving a response:
except Exception as e: print(f"Warning: detect_query_type failed, defaulting to 'general' -- {e}") return "general"

This database write is wrapped in a try/except block so that a logging failure whether caused by a network error, a Supabase outage, or a schema mismatch never prevents the student from receiving their answer:

Supabase Clients Two separate Supabase clients are initialized in config.py with different keys:
supabase=create_client(SUPABASE_URL, SUPABASE_KEY)# anon key supabase_admin=create_client(SUPABASE_URL, SUPABASE_SERVICE_KEY)#service role key
· The supabase client uses the anon key and is used only for authentication operations such as verifying tokens.
· The supabase_admin client uses the service role key and is used for all direct database table reads and writes.
pg. 111

This separation is necessary for Row Level Security (RLS) compatibility, the anon key is subject to RLS policies while the service role key bypasses RLS on the server side. Since all database operations originate from the controlled backend environment, it is safe to use the service key for data access while keeping the anon key scoped to authentication only.
Cross-Origin Resource Sharing (CORS) Restriction Cross-origin requests to the backend are restricted to a single allowed origin, configured through the environment variable at startup:
CORS(app, origins=[os.getenv("FRONTEND_URL", "http://localhost:3000")])
This implies that only requests originating from the configured frontend URL are permitted. All other origins are blocked at the CORS layer before reaching any route handler.
SQL Injection Prevention All database operations use the Supabase client's built-in query builder methods such as
.select(), .insert(), .update(), and .eq(). These methods use parameterized queries internally, meaning user input is never concatenated directly into a SQL string. This eliminates the risk of SQL injection across all database interactions in the system.
Prompt Injection Prevention Prompt injection occurs when a user crafts input designed to override or manipulate the system
instructions sent to the LLM. Two measures are in place to mitigate this:
1. The 1,000 character limit applied in validate_query() restricts the size of any injected payload before it reaches the AI pipeline.
2. The system prompt and user input are passed as structurally separate messages in the OpenAI API call using distinct roles:
messages = [ ChatMessage(role="system", content=GPA_SYSTEM_PROMPT), *history, ChatMessage(role="user", content=query),
]
Because the system instructions and the user message occupy separate roles in the API call, user input cannot override or escape the system prompt regardless of its content.

Current Limitations
The system is functional, but several limitations remain at this stage of development:
pg. 115

· LlamaParse subscript digits: LlamaParse outputs subscript Unicode digits () instead of standard digits. This is resolved through a post-processing normalization step in ingest.py but may require monitoring if new document formats are added.
· No automated content updates: While the web scraper (Firecrawl) and social media scraper (Apify) are fully implemented and operational, web content ingestion is triggered manually by an authorized administrator through the admin panel. Social media sources (Instagram) are automatically re-scraped on a weekly schedule using APScheduler, which detects and ingests only new posts while skipping previously processed content. Extending automated scheduling to web sources was considered out of scope for the current implementation and is planned as a future enhancement.
· Cross-lingual retrieval accuracy: The vector search performs best when the query language matches the document language. When a student submits a query in one language (example: English) against documents stored in another (example: Arabic), the semantic similarity scores are lower, reducing retrieval accuracy. This is a characteristic of the textembedding-3-small model used for vectorization. The effect is more pronounced for shortform content such as social media posts, while longer structured documents are retrieved more reliably across languages.

Conclusion & Supporting Materials

Conclusion
This project addressed a well-documented challenge faced by Kuwait University students: the fragmentation of academic and administrative information across multiple platforms, leading to reliance on informal channels and repetitive inquiries to staff. The result is Daleel KU, a bilingual AI-enabled academic chatbot that provides students with accurate, centralized access to university information without requiring login or registration.
The system delivers a RAG-based conversational interface grounded in verified official KU sources, with bilingual support in Arabic and English, GPA estimation, source-transparent responses, automated content ingestion from websites, PDFs, and social media, along with an administrative control panel for knowledge base management and query log oversight.
While current limitations exist, these present clear directions for future work, including automated web content scheduling, improved cross-lingual retrieval through multilingual embedding models, and expansion of the knowledge base to additional Kuwait University colleges beyond CLS. Daleel KU shows that a reliable, bilingual, and institutionally governed academic assistant can be built within a semester timeline and provides a replicable model for other universities across Kuwait and the broader Gulf region.
